# Breaking the Mold: Redefining Executive Presence with Authenticity

"Executive Presence." For many professionals—especially those from technical backgrounds or underrepresented cultural groups—this term often feels like a barrier. It conjures images of loud voices, expensive suits, and an aggressive command of the room.

However, at a recent career panel hosted by Rise Together, a group of seasoned leaders from top-tier tech and consumer goods companies gathered to dismantle this stereotype. Their consensus was clear: Presence isn't about performing a character; it is about clarity, trust, and the courage to be yourself.

---

## 1. Redefining the Term: It's Not Just About "Looking the Part"

When the floor was opened to define executive presence, the answers moved far beyond the traditional "ABC" model (Appearance, Behavior, Communication). Instead, the conversation anchored on trust and clarity.

- **Trustworthiness:** A Software Engineering Manager noted that presence is essentially personal branding. It is about building enough credibility that people trust you to deliver on goals.
- **Clarity and Determination:** The panel host defined it as knowing exactly what you want and having the determination to execute it. You don't need a C-suite title to have presence; you simply need a clear vision.
- **Owning the Unknown:** A Senior Product Manager from Meta highlighted that presence is also about "owning your voice" even when you are afraid. It is the ability to navigate the unknown without letting fear paralyze your leadership.

---

## 2. Turning Weaknesses into Weapons: Stories from the Field

The most powerful takeaways came from the panelists' vulnerability. They shared how they turned perceived liabilities—like language barriers or introversion—into their greatest assets.

### The "Whiteboard Strategy": Overcoming Language Barriers

A Principal Engineer Lead at Microsoft shared a pivotal moment from her transition from Shanghai to the U.S. Struggling with English and finding it hard to interject in fast-paced meetings with senior stakeholders, she felt her presence slipping.

- **The Pivot:** Her manager advised her to "use your strengths to solve what you are not good at."
- **The Strategy:** Instead of trying to win a verbal debate, she walked to the whiteboard and drew system diagrams.
- **The Result:** The visual aid instantly clarified complex issues, shifting the focus from her language skills to her architectural logic. She realized presence wasn't about being the loudest talker, but about driving the result.

### The "Quiet" Leader: Rejecting Performative Leadership

A Commercial Leader at P&G recounted receiving feedback early in her career that she was "too quiet." Initially, she tried to force herself to speak more, but she soon realized that constant talking is often a sign of arrogance, not leadership.

- **The Pivot:** She distinguished between "true leadership" and "performative leadership."
- **The Strategy:** She leaned into her nature as an observer. In business reviews, she "worked the room" by reading the audience's non-verbal cues and intentionally passing the microphone to subject-matter experts.
- **The Result:** She proved that you can command a room by empowering others rather than dominating them.

---

## 3. Strategic Career Management: Job vs. Career

A significant portion of the discussion focused on the mindset required to build long-term presence. The panelists drew a sharp distinction between a "job" and a "career."

- **The Distinction:** A "job" is your title on LinkedIn; it is a transaction to pay the bills. A "career" is who you are. It includes your personal values, your reputation, and your growth over a lifetime.
- **The Advice:** Do not let a single toxic environment define your worth. If a company's culture burns you out, remember that you can change jobs without losing your career. As one speaker noted, "Your career is you, not your job."

### From Individual Contributor (IC) to Manager

For those looking to transition into leadership, the panel offered specific tactics:

- **It's a Role, Not Just a Reward:** Moving to management isn't just a promotion; it's a change in profession. You move from deep technical work to broad communication and coordination.
- **Demonstrate Before You Ask:** Don't wait for the title. Start mentoring interns, leading "lunch and learns," or coordinating across teams. Show that you can align people toward a common goal.
- **Express Intent Early:** Tell your manager 5–10 years in advance that you are interested in leadership so they can place you in "pre-management" roles, like being a Scrum Lead.

---

## 4. The Art of Influence: Managing Up and Across

How do you push an innovative idea or a "startup-style" project in a large, bureaucratic company? The panel warned against going straight to the top.

- **Don't Pitch the VP Cold:** Going directly to a VP with a new idea is often a mistake because you don't know their specific pain points yet.
- **The "Cafeteria Strategy":** Build influence from the bottom up. One speaker suggested befriending the executive's assistant or team members in the cafeteria to understand what problems the leader is desperate to solve.
- **Solve the Burning Problem:** Frame your innovation not as "my great idea," but as the solution to the specific problem the leadership is already trying to fix.

---

## 5. Interviewing Your Future Manager

Perhaps the most radical advice on maintaining executive presence was regarding the interview process. Presence begins before you get the job.

- **Interview Them Back:** One speaker, a former recruiter, emphasized that an interview is a two-way street. She recommended asking, "Why should I work for you?" This establishes self-worth immediately.
- **The "Parent Test":** She also suggested a psychological check: Does this manager remind you of a parent you have a difficult relationship with? If so, run. You are likely to repeat old patterns that will hinder your success.
- **Assess the Fit:** Use the interview to determine if the manager's goals for the next 2–3 years align with yours. The quality of your direct manager is the single biggest determinant of your work environment.

---

## 6. Actionable Takeaways for Tomorrow

To wrap up, the leaders offered three simple habits to build presence immediately:

- **Tailor Your Story:** Never deliver a generic update. Prepare different versions of your message depending on whether you are talking to an engineer, a product manager, or a VP.
- **The Power of the Pause:** Speak slower. Use silence intentionally. A pause allows the audience to digest your point and signals that you are confident enough to hold the space.
- **Give Credit:** Secure leaders shine the light on others. Publicly acknowledging your team members builds trust and presence.
